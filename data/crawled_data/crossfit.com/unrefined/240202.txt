Champions Club Scaling Notes
RANT
Quick aside, I remember doing this when it first came out and was convinced I'd never be able to feel my forearms again. Anyway, the 20-year anniversary CrossFit Journal article this time around is 
Macroclimbing
, and in my opinion it's the second-most underrated article from the first generation (What About Recovery being #1). There is so much great insight on the importance of hanging, swinging, unstructured/informal movement, and play, and also the benefit of getting to know local businesses on a personal basis, but maaaaaannn I miss a CrossFit that was willing to talk like this:
On average, the Bachar ladder is a huge hit with our crew. The ladders are going to serve as a powerful incentive to good nutrition; the fat boys are less than thrilled.
Also, I'd like to talk about football for a minute. And not my lizard-brain addiction to college football, but my experience playing football. My mom and dad made me play starting in 5th grade and I played all the way through my senior year in high school (save for 7th and 9th grade due to concussions). I was the quarterback all through my career - which meant I got to sit out of a lot of hitting drills in practice, but still got absolutely hamblasted in games. There were many things I liked about football, the most prominent of which was it toughened me up for basketball; I noticed significant improvements as a defender and rebounder coming out of football seasons, and noticed a lack of it in the two seasons where I didn't play football before. That first practice with pads was a rude wake-up call to 9 months of non-violence, but as the season went on I just became more and more accustomed to the contact.
I'm saying all of that to lead into my second favorite part of Macroclimbing:
Requiring the ladder climbers to traverse the cargo net at ladder‚Äôs top and cross each other on the net to descend on the opposite rope is fun to watch through your fingers. Allowing competitors physical contact on the traverse is in the cards. We‚Äôll not go into the ideas for games that have been tabled and shelved since installing the climbing network. More than a few ideas involve throwing medicine balls at climbers.
So... yeah. I promise I get it. As most of you know I own my own affiliate, and it is literally the only thing I have; no degree, no side-jobs, just the gym. I know what it's like to watch Wesley fall from the pull-up bar or Mama V slide down from the rope too fast. So why on earth would I encourage 
more
 danger than we are already exposed to as the price to be paid for having not sat on our ass? Well, one of the things I value the most about a person is toughness, and while not everyone has to be a rugby player like Evan to be in the Champions Club, you can't be soft. Are we going to throw a medicine ball at you while you do pull-ups? No, we are not. Is it likely someone is going to give you a friendly shove during floor hockey or bear crawls back on the turf? Absolutely! Am I going to make everyone climb to the tall pull-up bars on the garage door like the babies at the gym? No, I am not. Am I going to insist everyone hang or do pull-ups on a high bar every now and then? Absolutely.
I think it's important to add a healthy dose of fear and contact to the CrossFit routine. That can be Mrs. Gloria climbing a snow mound at 81 years old or Mr. Curtis doing a handstand rollout or going to the park and playing "touch" football or doing handstand wars against the wall. Spotters, mats, progression, and scaling are obviously key here, as risking a major injury over a gimmick is not a good tradeoff. But ask yourself, when is the last time you were genuinely scared for your safety when you were in the gym? When you were 
preeeetttttyyyy
 sure you were going to be okay, but not completely sure. Doesn't have to be a backflip; it can be as simple as a somersault or a legless rope climb, if that's where you're at. Or allowing a dog or a child to "interfere" with your next sit-up set. All I know is CrossFit isn't necessarily supposed to prepare us for CrossFit, but rather the unknown and unknowable. We don't need to build our program around Macroclimbing but it might not be a bad idea to sub a tree climb for pull-ups in a workout every once in a while.
Welp, that was a much longer rant than expected.

Heroes who have served us, especially Corporal Erin Doyle. Also, people who are probably good at macroclimbing
